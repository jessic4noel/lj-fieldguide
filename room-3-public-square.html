Room III — Public Sphere: The Platform for Journalism, Political Mobilization, and Opposition

posted by: jess_ru

Alexei Navalny on the upcoming Coordination Council elections. The original entry follows. Curator commentary in the comments below.

jess_ru2026-04-29 · 15:12
By the late 2000s, LiveJournal had become something more architectural than a website. In media scholar Ilya Kiriya's framing, the Russian media system can be understood as the coexistence of a "main public sphere" — national television, state-aligned radio, certain political print outlets — and a "parallel public sphere" composed of institutionalized opposition media such as Ekho Moskvy and REN-TV, alongside non-institutionalized digital spaces, foremost among them blogs and social networking sites. Within this parallel sphere, ЖЖ held a peculiar centrality. It was not the only platform Russians used, but it was the one on which the most serious political and intellectual work was done. The Berkman Center's 2010 mapping of the Russian-language blogosphere — some 65 million blogs in total — found a central, politically engaged core composed predominantly of LJ accounts. Of the roughly 2.8 million LJ accounts active at the time, the platform was generating approximately 90,000 entries daily.

What the platform enabled, more than anything, was a "blogization" of Russian journalism — a politicizing of the media and social space that allowed institutionalized writers to operate outside the editorial constraints, soft and hard, of their employers. Where a journalist's print or broadcast outlet imposed limits — whether through formal censorship or the subtler self-censorship that comes from working inside a state-aligned media ecology — the LJ blog provided a parallel surface. Stories too sharp for the front page could be argued for in a journal post. Sources unwilling to speak on the record could be honored in a comment thread. LiveJournal did not replace institutional journalism; it ran alongside it as the journalist's compensatory medium — a place where the writer could be fully professional and fully political at once. Olessia Koltsova and Andrey Shcherbak's empirical work on the 2011–2012 political blogosphere is the most rigorous account of this double life.

The political star most fully made on ЖЖ was Alexei Navalny's. Navalny entered public life as a blogger before he was a candidate; his early audience — the people who would later fill Bolotnaya Square — was assembled here, post by post, on documentation of procurement fraud at state-owned companies and, beginning in December 2011, the electoral falsifications that prompted the largest protest wave in two decades. His August 13, 2012 post on the upcoming Coordination Council elections is the platform in its full politico-organizational mode: a public explanation of the Central Election Committee's structure, the names of Anton Nossik and Yandex co-founder Ilya Segalovich among its members, and an embedded 30-minute video lecture. Interestingly, his rise corresponded almost exactly with the end of what political scientists have called the "apolitical era" of the 2000s and the beginning of a new protest politics that would define the next decade. It was on LJ that Navalny first articulated the tactical-voting strategy that would later become Smart Voting — initially formulated as "Vote for any party except United Russia" — and it was on LJ that he assembled, in the form of comment-threaded evidence, the documentary record of fraud through which his analysis took on the weight of journalism rather than commentary.

If Room I argues that ЖЖ's architecture was hospitable to Russian intellectual life, Room III argues that this hospitality became, between roughly 2008 and 2012, politically consequential. The platform was not merely a venue for opposition. For a brief and remarkable interval, as Sergey Sanovich, Denis Stukal, and Joshua Tucker have shown empirically in their Appendix B, it was the opposition's infrastructure.
