Room IV — Capture: How the Platform Became Hollowed Ground

posted by: jess_ru

Alexei Kungurov's "Кого на самом деле бомбят путинские соколы," posted to the captured-era LiveJournal interface a year before his arrest. The original entry follows. Curator commentary in the comments below.

Curator's commentary  (4 comments)

jess_ru2026-04-29 · 15:32
On 12 October 2015, the Tyumen-based blogger Alexei Kungurov posted to his ЖЖ a methodical, technically detailed debunking of the Kremlin's seventh myth justifying the Russian military intervention in Syria — the myth that if ISIS prevailed, its forces would "pour into Russia." Kungurov walked through the geography. Russia is separated from Syria by Turkey and Georgia. To "pour," ISIS would have to overrun a 77-million-person country defended by NATO's second-largest army, which it could not even reach without first overrunning the 700,000-strong Iraqi anti-jihadist formations and the 200,000-strong Kurdish Peshmerga, the latter equipped with as many as 250 tanks. The post is patient, sourced, devastating. Strikingly, it is also published on an interface that no longer looks like a journal. Flat-grey, with share buttons for Facebook, VK, WhatsApp, and Telegram; a heart icon and a numerical comment counter where the threaded LJ-blue reply used to be. The platform itself looks captured before the writer does.

The capture happened in three overlapping registers — ownership, infrastructure, and law — and it took roughly a decade. At the end of 2007, the Russian-based media company SUP, founded by the American entrepreneur Andrew Paulson and the Kremlin-connected banker Alexander Mamut, purchased LiveJournal from Six Apart for approximately $30 million, in what was the largest Russian takeover of a U.S. internet company to that point. Russian bloggers responded with immediate alarm. As one user with the handle halph wrote, the Kremlin had "something to fear" from LJ, "the only platform for free speech in Russia." Almost simultaneously came the technical war. The first major attack arrived on 6 August 2009 — the first anniversary of the Russo-Georgian war — and targeted the pro-Georgian blogger cyxymu; it was strong enough to disrupt Facebook and shut down Twitter and LiveJournal entirely. The pattern recurred. On the weekend of the December 2011 Duma elections, two dozen of the most prominent independent media outlets — The New Times, Kommersant, Echo of Moscow, Novaya Gazeta — and the entire LiveJournal platform were shut down for hours. So were the election monitors' coordinating portals, foremost among them GOLOS.

If 2009 to 2012 was the era of denial-of-service attacks, the years that followed were the era of statute. In July 2012, despite vocal protests, the State Duma adopted, and Vladimir Putin signed, Federal Law No. 139-FZ — the Internet Restriction Bill — which created a continuously updated Russian Internet Blacklist administered by Roskomnadzor. In May 2014, Putin signed a further law requiring any blogger with a daily readership above 3,000 to register with the government, reveal their identity, archive their posts, and follow the news-production laws governing the press during election campaigns. Amendments to Article 280.1 of the Criminal Code made the use of "telecommunication networks (including 'Internet')" an aggravating factor in any prosecution for public appeals to violate Russia's territorial integrity. On 13 March 2014, at the height of the Crimea crisis, Grani.ru, Kasparov.ru, EJ.ru, and Alexei Navalny's LiveJournal were blocked by all Russian ISPs by government order — without specific notice of what content had triggered the block.

In December 2016, practically overnight, the LJ servers were physically relocated from California to Russia. The relocation meant that LJ could no longer refuse to hand over user data to the Russian state, as it had been doing from California. That same month, Kungurov was sentenced to two years in a penal colony settlement for his post on Putin's "falcons" in Syria — his criticism of the military campaign reclassified by investigators as "justification of ISIS activities." He was later transferred to a real prison, released in 2018, and immediately left Russia, citing renewed threats from law enforcement. By April 2017, SUP had released a new terms of service classifying any blog with over 3,000 daily viewers as a media outlet subject to censorship and prosecution, and permitting the Russian government to pull any content it deemed "obscene." Most remaining users left the platform. The argument of this room, then, is that what was done to ЖЖ was not a ban. Bans leave 404s. Capture leaves an empty room you can still walk into. The platform still works. Kungurov's post is, as of 2026, still online. But the writer who posted it spent two years in a penal colony for writing posts of this kind, and the platform on which he wrote is, in every sense that matters, no longer the platform Russian intellectuals knew. Hollowed ground.
