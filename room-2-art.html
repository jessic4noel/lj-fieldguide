Room II Art and Culture: A Lively Scene of Poetry and Artistic Community

posted by: jess_ru

Kirill Medvedev's "толерантность не катит," posted under the username zoltan_partosh. The original entry follows. Curator commentary in the comments below.

Curator's commentary  (4 comments)

jess_ru2026-04-29 · 14:52
On 20 May 2008, at 11:19 PM, the poet Kirill Medvedev — writing under the username zoltan_partosh, his blog crowned by the red flag of his publishing imprint Свободное марксистское издательство (Free Marxist Press) — posted a five-sentence entry titled "толерантность не катит" (tolerance doesn't fly). A child asks his father whether God exists. The father gives the relativist answer his moment requires: some believe, some don't. The child, unimpressed, presses again: yes, but does He actually exist? The post closes with a single sentence of judgment: "In my opinion, a hopeless situation, for today." This is the form Medvedev's LJ archive will take, year after year — a domestic scene that hardens into a one-line political conclusion, a prose vignette that operates as a poem, a poem that operates as a position.

jess_ru2026-04-29 · 14:56
The post is not, in any obvious sense, an artwork. And yet it is the genre on which Medvedev built one of the most consequential bodies of work of his generation. After his second book, he began what would become a series of disavowals — refusing copyright on his published work, withdrawing from the institutions allocating Russia's cultural capital, devoting himself instead to his LiveJournal, to political essays, to activism, to actions including a one-man picket outside a commercial production of Brecht that ended with Medvedev punched by the theater's security guard. The avant-garde gesture Medvedev was making — to live in the world as an artist without being an artist, without producing intellectual property — was made possible by ЖЖ. Posting freely, daily, in long form, alongside the anonymous graphomaniacs of the platform, he found a venue where the formal moves of a serious poetics could happen on the same page as the daily political statement, without one cannibalizing the other.

Medvedev's strategy was singular but not solitary. The literature scholar Roman Leibov, a Slavic studies lecturer at the University of Tartu, had logged into ЖЖ in 2001 with the entry "First attempt at writing. Let's try it in Russian… how funny!" — an inauguration that already understood itself as a literary act. "I liked it," Leibov later recalled, "because there had been a demand for something like this for a long time." Eugene Gorny, the literary critic and internet researcher, joined two months afterward. "At the time we didn't think of ourselves as pioneers," he said. "We were just enjoying ourselves." The community they helped build was, from the outset, the creative worker's blog in its most concentrated form: a professional producer of creativity who devotes substantial attention to creative production within the blog itself. Designers posted pictures of recent work, musicians shared insights into their process, visual artists alternated new pieces with essayistic musings, literary authors published new poetry and prose. ЖЖ's cultural cluster, which the Berkman Center's mapping of the Russian blogosphere would later measure at 39.7 percent of all activity, was not incidental to the platform. It was a substantial portion of its purpose.

Crucially, this writerly public did not remain decorative. By the mid-2000s, the post-Soviet consensus that poetry and politics should be kept apart had begun to collapse, prompting what scholars have called a reassessment of the traditions of civic and political poetry in Russia and a struggle to dethrone "apoliticism" as a self-evident norm. By 2011, when the protests against electoral fraud filled Bolotnaya Square, ЖЖ was not only the publication venue of choice for politicized writers. It was, as one scholar put it, the rostrum of the protest movement — the textual surface on which the revolution acquired its own narrative form. Strikingly, the literati's participation was as natural as it was consequential: they had been on ЖЖ for nearly a decade, writing daily, used to the tools, and they continued writing through the protests, into the protests, as the protests. The argument of this room is that ЖЖ's architecture made a specifically writerly public possible, and that this is what later platforms could not replicate. Medvedev's five-sentence post is not a fragment from a vanished tradition. It is a record of the moment when, on a single American blogging platform, a Russian poet could be a poet, an activist, a domestic father, and a publisher at once.
