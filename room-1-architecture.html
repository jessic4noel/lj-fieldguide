Room I — Adoption: How a Small American Platform Fit Russian Intellectual Habits

posted by: jess_ru

The first Cyrillic post on LiveJournal. The original entry follows. Curator commentary in the comments below.

Curator's commentary  (4 comments)

jess_ru2026-04-29 · 14:32
At 3:27 PM on 30 November 1999, a user by the name of "at" discovered that the popular American blogging platform LiveJournal supported Cyrillic. The site's very first Russian-language post — "I dazhe po-russki mozhno? Nu vaashche" [Wait, you can even post in Russian? Waoaoaoaaahhhh] — announces the new possibility in an idiom already native to the medium. Strikingly, the misspelling of voobshche anticipates the non-normative spellings that would soon define the early Russian internet, eventually canonized as Padonskii or Olbanskii ("падонский/олбанский йезыг"). The first Cyrillic post on ЖЖ is already, in miniature, what ЖЖ would become: confessional, playful, slightly subversive, linguistically inventive.

LiveJournal's format made room for both intimacy and reach. Its long-form confessional style suited readers and writers already accustomed to journals, letters, and samizdat; its social features — tagging, following, threaded response — offered the kind of networked audience-building that would come to characterize Web 2.0. Within a few years of "at's" first post, the platform had acquired what Andrei Podshibiakin called the informal status of "an area for Russian intellectuals," gradually becoming a national cultural phenomenon. Poets, journalists, scholars, and activists alike found a home there. Among them was Vera Polozkova (b. 1986), today by most measures the most-read poet in Russia, whose career began on the platform.

Why ЖЖ, and not another? The reasons Russian journalists offered when asked, years later, were strikingly consistent. The platform's communicative power was paramount: Vladimir Varfolomeev of Ekho Moskvy wrote of his five thousand subscribers, "I can't simply drop and leave" — an audience built post by post and held there by the architecture itself. The format offered, in the words of Alexandr Podrabinek of RFI and Novaia Gazeta, something Twitter and Facebook simply could not: "It is more for communication than for information." "New rapid communications," Podrabinek argued, "can't replace depth of analysis and gravity of argument." Others echoed the same point. Irek Murtazin of Novaia Gazeta described the format as offering "broader opportunities for debates, for the development of opinions, for serious discussion." Aleksandr Malkevich, a provincial television journalist, called LiveJournal a "unique way to speak in a circumlocutory manner, and with illustrations."

What the journalists were defending, in effect, was a shape: the threaded, durable, long-form publication that LJ alone preserved. The Berkman Center's mapping of the Russian blogosphere confirmed that shape empirically — the Russian-language blogosphere was found to be significantly clustered, politically and socially engaged at its core, culturally and professionally elaborate at its edges. Of the topics analyzed, 48.7 percent were socio-political, 39.7 percent cultural, 2.1 percent professional, and 9.4 percent ambient or personal. Crucially, when journalists linked outward, they linked overwhelmingly to oppositional or alternative-agenda media — Ekho Moskvy, Grani.ru, Novaia Gazeta, Kommersant, Vedomosti — using LiveJournal as the connective tissue of a parallel public sphere. Adoption, then, was never about ЖЖ being merely available. It was about ЖЖ being the right shape for what Russian intellectuals already wanted to do. The Cyrillic post that opened the door in 1999 was, in retrospect, a hinge.
