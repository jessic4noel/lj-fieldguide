How a Foreign Object Took Root

posted by: jess_ru

mood: tentative  ·  music: dial-up handshake

tags: architecture, adoption, runet, nossik, leibov

Artist statement. The argument of this room is that LiveJournal's architecture — long-form posts, threaded comments, friend-of-friend graph, communities — was the reason a generation of Russian intellectuals settled on a foreign American platform as their public sphere. The artifacts below walk through that adoption, 1999–2005. Scroll to advance.

Artifact 5 · architecture
Why this platform, not another

LiveJournal

  • Long-form posts
  • Threaded comments
  • Friend-of-friend graph
  • Communities
  • Pseudonymous, durable
  • Chronological feed

Twitter (2009)

  • 140 characters
  • Flat replies
  • One-way follow
  • Hashtag venues
  • Real-name pressure
  • Algorithmic feed
The bones mattered. LJ produced literary publics; Twitter produced viral moments.

March 1999. Portland.

Brad Fitzpatrick, then a college freshman, builds LiveJournal so his high-school friends can keep up with him. It is a private object made for a small circle. He doesn't know yet that he's built a platform; he certainly doesn't know he's built Russia's most important public sphere of the 2000s.

Source: Brad Fitzpatrick, bradfitz.com (primary); historical context in Gorham, Lunde, & Paulsen 2014.

2001. The first Cyrillic posts.

Russian-language users find the platform. Russian-language internet (Runet) has at this point a strong literary culture inherited from samizdat, FidoNet, and email lists. LJ's long-form editor and threaded comments map directly onto habits these readers and writers already have.

Source: Wayback Machine; Lonkila & Gladarev 2016 on early Russian-language LJ communities.

Anton Nossik becomes dolboeb.

Nossik — already a working journalist — turns LJ into a daily column. His blog mixes media commentary, Israel/Russia politics, and ordinary daily writing. He shows the platform's power as professional infrastructure: a journalist with no editor, no print schedule, and a built-in readership.

Source: Nossik 2014, "I Helped Build Russia's Internet…" New Republic.

Roman Leibov logs in from Tartu.

Leibov, a literary scholar at the University of Tartu, joins LJ early and writes in it for the next two decades. He later argues, in academic work, that LJ became the host of a specifically literary public — one whose central genre was "occasional political poetry" written in response to events and circulated in comment threads.

Source: Leibov, "Occasional Political Poetry and the Culture of the Russian Internet," in Gorham, Lunde, & Paulsen 2014.

Why this platform, and not another.

The case the room makes: LJ's architecture was the cause. Long-form posts let people write at the length their thinking actually wanted. Threaded comments let readers argue with each other rather than only with the author. The friend-of-friend graph propagated posts through trusted intermediaries instead of algorithmic broadcast. Communities provided shared journals where strangers could converge around topics. None of these features are present in Twitter; only some are present in Facebook. The room ends here, with a comparison: the next room is what was built inside this architecture.

Original synthesis. Conceptual frame: Gorham, Lunde, & Paulsen 2014; Sanovich, Stukal, & Tucker 2018 on the platform's later political weight.
