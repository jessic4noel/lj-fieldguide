I

How a Foreign Object Took Root

posted by: jess_ru  ·  mood: tentative  ·  music: dial-up handshake

In 1999 a teenager in Portland built a diary platform for himself and his college friends. By 2003 a small but serious crowd of Russian intellectuals had quietly colonized it. This room is about why ЖЖ's architecture — long-form posts, threaded comments, friend-of-friend graph — fit Russian habits of intellectual exchange better than email lists or, later, Twitter. Featured: bradfitz.com primary materials, Anton Nossik (dolboeb), Roman Leibov (r_l).

II

The Living Room

posted by: jess_ru  ·  mood: prolific  ·  music: kitchen kettle, distant

A literary platform — possibly the only true one the Russian internet ever had. Roman Leibov on the tradition of occasional political poetry that thrived in LJ threads; Eugene Gorny on humor and jokes as a structuring social form; the wartime poetry continuum that runs through into 2022–. The room argues that LJ's architecture made a specifically writerly public possible, and that this is what later platforms could not replicate.

The Public Square

posted by: jess_ru  ·  mood: charged  ·  music: Bolotnaya Square, snow on a microphone

LJ as professional infrastructure for journalists and as opposition organizing. Navalny's election-fraud posts; the network shape of the Russian blogosphere; the moment the regime stopped ignoring the platform and started contesting it — Sanovich, Stukal, & Tucker (2018) Appendix B is the empirical anchor for that shift. This is the room where a public sphere acts politically, and where its adversaries learn its terrain.

Monday, May 1, 2017 — 09:15
IV

The Capture

posted by: jess_ru  ·  mood: hollowed  ·  music: server fans, fluorescent hum

How ЖЖ was emptied without being banned. The 2007 sale to SUP, absorption into the Rambler ecosystem, DDoS waves on opposition blogs, the 2014 bloggers' law, the relocation of servers to Russia, and the user exodus to Facebook and then Telegram. The room reconstructs each step as administrative weather rather than a single takedown — and asks what kind of capture this actually is.
