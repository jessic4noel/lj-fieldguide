Message from the Curator

posted by: jess_ru  · 

In 1999, a freshman computer science student at the University of Washington built an open source programming system to serve as an online diary. While published without initial intent, Brad Fitzpatrick's "LiveJournal" built Russia's most important public sphere. For around a decade 2001–2011, LiveJournal, most commonly referred to as LJ or ЖЖ in shorthand, served as the primary social networking site in Russia. By the turn of the first decade of the century, ЖЖ served as a platform rich with published expressions of life in Russia: poetry, humor, professional journalism, opposition politics. Interestingly, such immense publications spanning art, culture, and social-political lamentations are built, brick by brick (or rather post by post), a memoir, a reflection of the nation's political environment.

ЖЖ took over Russia because it archetecturally and jurdisticdionally aligned with user's needs. The Berkman Center's mapping of the Russian-language blogosphere found it to be significantly clustered into four discrete thematic discussion zones: political and social discourse (elections, opposition, current events), cultural matters (literature, music, theater, philosophy, fashion), professional matters (journalism, media ethics, censorship), and the ambient category of private writing and "messages about nothing." Each cluster held its own communities, logics, and lifeblood. Jurisdictionally, LiveJournal's servers were located in California, well outside the reach of a Russian state that praticed censorship and corporate solicitation for private user data. LifeJournal's user experience centering community and its geographical safety made ЖЖ the breeding ground for Russian intellectuals, activists, and journalists.

LiveJournal also served as a platform so politically significant, intellectuals continue to examine its importance. Anton Nossik, Russia's first blogger, used LJ to invent Russian-language internet journalism. Similarly, academic Roman Leibov pioneered the Russian-language LJ and a tradition of political poetry that thrived amongst ЖЖ threads. Another scholar, USSR-born Eugene Gorny, traced how the use of humor functioned as a distinct mode of social resistance. Alexei Navalny, Russian activist and opposition leader, built his early mobilization on the platform. After this bloom of life, however, ЖЖ was captured and emptied between 2007 and 2017 through a series of Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) cyberattacks, the ownership transfer to Russian state-aligned SUP Media and later Rambler, and Putin's restrictive Bloggers' Law.

This website serves as a media-based "field guide" of LiveJournal's uncanny arc. Specifically, it is an interactive web exhibition styled as a reflection of LiveJournal, paired with a series of curatorial essays. This website's landing page is laid out as a LiveJournal "friends feed" to bring the site back to life. On this landing page, four entries are displayed exactly as LiveJournal would have rendered them — each with a userpic, timestamp, entry title, and preview text. The user clicks on each post to walk through four "rooms," each mapping an era or epoch of LiveJournal.

Room I Adoption
Room II Art and Culture
Room III Public Sphere

These rooms each serve as their own "post," displaying real, historically relevant LJ posts as example artifacts and the "comments" underneath each post hold descriptive curatorial essays. These are a one-page artist statement for each of the four rooms, plus a brief introduction and closing note styled as blocked comments.
