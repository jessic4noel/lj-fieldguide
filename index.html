A Field Guide to ЖЖ

Russia's accidental public sphere, 1999–2024

curated by jess_ru · POLISCI 227R · advisor: sanovich

Summary All entries Sources About

Abstract

In 1999, a freshman computer science student at the University of Washington built an open source programming system to serve as an online diary. While published without initial intent, Brad Fitzpatrick's "LiveJournal" built Russia's most important public sphere. For around a decade 2001–2011, LiveJournal, most commonly referred to as LJ or ЖЖ in shorthand, served as the primary social networking site in Russia. By the turn of the first decade of the century, ЖЖ served as a platform rich with published expressions of life in Russia: poetry, humor, professional journalism, opposition politics. Interestingly, such immense publications spanning art, culture, and social-political lamentations are built, brick by brick (or rather post by post), a memoir, a reflection of the nation's political environment.

LiveJournal also served as a platform so politically significant, intellectuals continue to examine its importance. Anton Nossik, Russia's first blogger, used LJ to invent Russian-language internet journalism. Similarly, academic Roman Leibov pioneered the Russian-language LJ and a tradition of political poetry that thrived amongst ЖЖ threads. Another scholar, USSR-born Eugene Gorny, traced how the use of humor functioned as a distinct mode of social resistance. Alexei Navalny, Russian activist and opposition leader, built his early mobilization on the platform. After this bloom of life, however, ЖЖ was captured and emptied between 2007 and 2017 through a series of Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) cyberattacks, the ownership transfer to Russian state-aligned SUP Media and later Rambler, and Putin's restrictive Bloggers' Law.

I propose a media-based "field guide" of LiveJournal's uncanny arc. Specifically, I will build an interactive web exhibition styled as a reflection of LiveJournal, paired with a series of curatorial essays. The interactive web exhibition will be a static website — an HTML, CSS, and JavaScript coded site that opens in any web browser. The website's landing page is laid out as a LiveJournal "friends feed" to bring the site back to life. On this landing page, four entries are displayed exactly as LiveJournal would have rendered them — each with a userpic, timestamp, mood line, entry title, and preview text. The user clicks on each post to walk through four "rooms," each mapping an era or epoch of LiveJournal.

Room I — Adoption: How a small American platform fit Russian intellectual habits.
Room II — Art and Culture: A lively scene of poetry, humor, and artistic community.
Room III — Public Sphere: The platform for journalism, political mobilization, and opposition.
Room IV — Capture: How the platform became hollowed ground through DDoS attacks, ownership change, and law.

These rooms each serve as their own scroll-driven page, which I intend to build with the Knight Lab scrollytelling library Scrollama — a static-site library where artifacts and curatorial captions reveal in sequence as the visitor scrolls through the builder's narrative. The digital exhibition is paired with descriptive curatorial essays: a one-page artist statement for each of the four rooms, plus a brief introduction and closing note.

In 1999 a teenager in Portland built a diary platform for himself and his college friends. By 2003 a small but serious crowd of Russian intellectuals had quietly colonized it. This room is about why ЖЖ's architecture — long-form posts, threaded comments, friend-of-friend graph — fit Russian habits of intellectual exchange better than email lists or, later, Twitter. Roman Leibov on the tradition of occasional political poetry that thrived in LJ threads; Eugene Gorny on humor and jokes as a structuring social form; the wartime poetry continuum that runs through into 2022–. The Capture

posted by: jess_ru  ·  mood: hollowed  ·  music: server fans, fluorescent hum

How ЖЖ was emptied without being banned. The 2007 sale to SUP, absorption into the Rambler ecosystem, DDoS waves on opposition blogs, the 2014 bloggers' law, the relocation of servers to Russia, and the user exodus to Facebook and then Telegram. The room reconstructs each step as administrative weather rather than a single takedown — and asks what kind of capture this actually is.
